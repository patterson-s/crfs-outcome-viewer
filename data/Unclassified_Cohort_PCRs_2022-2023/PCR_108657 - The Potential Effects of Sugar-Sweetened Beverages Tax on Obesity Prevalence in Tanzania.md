# Project Highlights 

What are the five most important results (outputs or outcomes) of this project and how have they been useful or innovative?

New knowledge on NCDs in Tanzania
The project produced novel evidence about the economic cost of NCDs in Tanzania and developed a mathematical model to estimate the impact that a $20 \%$ tax on sugar sweetened beverages (SSBs) will have on obesity and government revenue. The project found that the economic burden that NCDs place on the public health system in Tanzania almost doubled between 2015 and 2019. It also found that a $20 \%$ tax will reduce obesity levels by about $7 \%$ and almost double the revenue that the Government currently raises from excise taxes on SSBs.

Policy influence
The project team has made initial policy impact through an invited brief that was distributed to all government ministers. This brief is known as the Swahili Policy Brief and contributions to its content are by special invitation only. The project team were privileged to have been selected to present their work in the Swahili Policy Brief following a dissemination workshop that they organized. This invitation underlies the strong impression that the project findings made on the participants at the dissemination workshop which included representatives of the Ministry of Health, Tanzania Commission for Science and Technology, among others.

## Gender implications

This project found that SSB taxation is not likely to be gender neutral. Even though Tanzanian women have higher levels of obesity than men, a 20\% SSB tax will led to more than two times a reduction in obesity among men as compared to women. Policymakers and stakeholders will need to keep this in mind as they consider fiscal policy reforms and other complimentary interventions for NCD risk prevention in Tanzania.

Project Outcomes
How does the project contribute to the field of study / research area?
Why was this research important to fund? What were the key scientific findings? What occurred as a result of this project?

The main goal of this project was to estimate the potential impact of tax on sugar sweetened beverages (SSB) on the prevalence of obesity. A secondary goal was to estimate how much additional revenue the Government of Tanzania could generate from this tax. Fiscal policies such as taxation have been recommended by the World Health Organization as cost-effective strategies for addressing NCDs. The key findings are as follows:

The economic burden and cost of NCDs is on the rise
The project found that the average annual economic cost of having an NCD amounts to US\$ 1,212 per person in 2019 which was more than the country's GDP per capita (US\$1,086 in 2019). This includes direct medical costs, direct non-medical costs such as travel and accommodation costs, and indirect costs such as income losses of individual patients. This amounts to a national aggregate cost of all NCD patients of US $\$ 349$ million in 2019. Further, NCDs cost the public health care system US\$281 million in 2019 compared to US $\$ 143$ million in 2015.

Taxation will reduce obesity levels
Using a mathematical model, the project found that levying a 20\% SSB tax in Tanzania will reduce obesity by $6.6 \%$ overall, and by $12.9 \%$ and $5.2 \%$ in adult males and adult females respectively. The SSB tax will have the strongest impact on adults in the age groups 15 to 34 and 55 to 64 years.

The model assumes that the beverage industry will pass 100\% of this tax increase to consumers. Therefore, a 20\% tax on SSBs will amount to a specific tax of TZS 294 (US\$0.13) per litre of soft drinks, TZS 326 (US\$0.14) per litre for energy drinks and TZS 307 (US\$0.13) per litre for sugar sweetened juices. Currently the specific tax on these products are TZS 61 (US\$0.026), TZS 58 (US\$0.025), TZS 9 (US\$0.004), and TZS 232 (US\$0.10), per litre for soft drinks, energy drinks, locally produced sugar sweetened juices and imported sugar sweetened juices respectively.

Government can expect significant revenue from taxation
Tanzania already has an excise tax on SSBs. The total amount of revenue it collects from this tax is TZS 416 billion (US\$ 178 million). By levying a 20\% SSB tax, total tax revenue will rise by 109\% to TZS 868 billion (US $\$ 372$ million). The project also estimated the administrative cost of implementing the tax. They found that it would only cost the Government of Tanzania around TZS 69,120,669 (US\$29,659) to implement a 20\% SSB tax - with about half of this being a one-off cost of passing tax reform legislation

Investing in knowledge and innovation for large scale change:
This project is the first of its kind in Eastern Africa that provides evidence of the potential impact that a tax on SSBs will have on obesity. The World Health Organization recommends fiscal policy interventions such as taxation as one of several tools aimed at addressing the rising global burden of NCDs. The evidence generated from this study is well positioned to influence policymakers in Tanzania to implement policy reforms that will be beneficial to the health of Tanzania and generate additional revenue that could be invested in NCD prevention activities.

Build the leaders for today and tomorrow:
The project provided an opportunity for a woman researcher Prof Fortunate Makene and her co-investigators to position themselves as leading voices on the issue of fiscal policy for NCD prevention in Sub Saharan Africa.
