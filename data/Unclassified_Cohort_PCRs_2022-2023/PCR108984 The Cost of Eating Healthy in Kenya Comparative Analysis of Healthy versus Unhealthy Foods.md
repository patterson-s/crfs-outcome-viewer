# Project Highlights 

What are the five most important results (outputs or outcomes) of this project and how have they been useful or innovative?

Generated new knowledge and enhanced visibility:
As far as we know, this project is the first of its kind to quantitatively assess the cost of eating healthy at a national level in Kenya. Four manuscripts are in the pipeline and are aiming to be published in open access journals. In addition, there is one policy brief, one video animation, and one blog post that are publicly available. The findings of this project were also featured in an article published by a major media outlet in Kenya. These outputs will serve to bolster the APHRC's standing as one of the foremost research institutions in Africa that focuses on the nexus between the food environment and health.

Informed policy dialogues:
During the dissemination events, policymakers and other stakeholders provided valuable feedback to the project team. In summary, the stakeholders appreciated the study results and confirmed that the evidence generated would influence their programs and activities. They also expressed their intention to use these findings to promote healthy food in various sectors, including advocating for more funding to be allocated to the agriculture sector. However, they also called for future studies that focus on sub-national level data, such as county-level data, since health is a devolved government function in Kenya. The stakeholders suggested that such sub-national data would produce evidence that is more likely to lead to action at the grassroots level.

## Gender implications:

A gendered analysis showed that compared to households led by men, a greater proportion of those led by women were able to adhere to the World Health Organization's recommended fruit and vegetable intake. However, women-led households that met the recommended intake of fruits and vegetables spent almost USD 100 more (per person annually) than male-led households that also met the same recommendations. The existence of these gender differences suggests that policy and program interventions aimed at improving the affordability of healthy diets should consider the potential gender implications.

Eating healthy is associated with higher socioeconomic status
One of the main result indicators in this study is the Healthy Diet Index (HDI). The HDI is an indicator of how closely dietary patterns adhere to World Health Organization recommendations on healthy diets. A higher HDI score indicates better adherence to these recommendations. The study found that households of higher socioeconomic status have relatively higher HDI scores than poorer households. For example, the mean HDI score was 0.46 for the wealthiest households in Kenya versus -0.02 for the poorest households, showing a positive correlation between wealth and healthier dietary patterns. In fact, regardless of whether the household was headed by a man or woman or located in an urban or rural area, the study found that wealthier households tended to consume a healthier diet than poorer households.

A healthy diet comes at a higher price

--- Page 2 ---

The study showed that the majority (84\%) of the households assessed were only able to meet four or fewer (out of nine) of the World Health Organization's healthy diet recommendations. However, the households that were able to score relatively high (meeting 6 or more of the dietary recommendations) had an average spending of USD 576 per person per year, while those who were not able to meet any of the recommendations had an average spending of around USD 481 per person per year. In addition, households that complied with the recommended intake of fruits and vegetables spent a significantly higher amount (USD 823) per person annually, compared to those who did not comply with the recommendations (USD 413).

Women-led households consumed healthier diets
There were no significant differences between women-led and men-led households in terms of HDI score. Nonetheless, a significant finding was that a higher percentage of women-led households (50\%) were able to meet the recommended fruit and vegetable intake in comparison to households led by men (43\%). It is worth noting that women-led households that were able to fulfill the recommended intake of fruits and vegetables spent an average of nearly USD 100 more (per person annually) than male-led households that met the same recommendations.
