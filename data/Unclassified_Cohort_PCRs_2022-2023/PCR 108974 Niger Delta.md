Project Highlights
What are the five most important results (outputs or outcomes) of this project and how have they been useful or innovative?

New knowledge generated and documented from a highly fragile region on drivers of vulnerabilities and climate-change impacts on rural households, particularly on women farmers, and how they cope. The study took place in three main ecological zones of the Niger Delta region-mangrove swamp (Delta South Senatorial District), freshwater swamp (Delta Central Senatorial District), and lowland rainforest (Delta North Senatorial District)â€”and included an extensive collection of primary data on household vulnerability and current and potential climate-related risks to agricultural activities based on experiences of loss, levels of concern, perceptions of change, and the identification of common practices and strategies to manage climate-related risks as well as needs for adaptation. They found that women's and men's capacities for building disaster and climate resilience were shaped by their social, cultural, economic, and natural resource management roles. Information came from a baseline survey of 4,000 households, interviews with 107 key informants, 30 focus group discussions (11 to 15 participants, comprising all men, all women, or a mix of men and women), and 3 feedback meetings with communities. The project also included an assessment of the impact of COVID-19 on local food production and informal food markets based on a survey of 2,000 farmers (comprising 600 food producers, 600 food marketers, and 800 households). The results of both studies are useful because Niger Delta's climate-related disasters, and other disasters such as COVID-19, are understudied, this region is hard to access and investigate, and existing research lacks adequate data and focuses on isolated environmental degradation issues. Niger Delta's fragile contexts, where infrastructure is weak or non-existent, create special challenges for collecting reliable data, for example, the lack of statistics registries that measure the basic demographic information essential to planning when disasters happen. There are also enormous security problems involved in conducting research in such contexts. Almost no attention has been paid to rural communities, especially during the region's period of conflict and violence.

More than 250 women farmers empowered through capacity building in 10 pilot communities within the three ecological zones. Engaging women in the communities was a major challenge because traditionally women are not given space to speak. To allow the women to speak more comfortably, the team held informal group discussions and visited the spaces where women live and work. The team

also created Community-based Project Implementation Committees (CPICs) in each targeted community, drawing from existing functional social/community groups, to address daily activities. Two or three leaders of these social groups were nominated to form the CPICs (ensuring at least one woman per CPIC), which also included Local Government Areas (LGAs) representatives (mainly men). Through the CPIC, a group of 25 women farmers in each of the 10 pilot communities were trained on crop diversification/production, construction of flood control channels, different irrigation methods, the fencing of ponds and fish traps, and land assessment techniques. The CPICs were responsible for monitoring the trainings. The trained community groups were also supported by the LGAs with various farm inputs to put the learning into practice through small community-based pilot interventions championed by women farmers. These inputs included fertilizers, improved groundnut seedlings, early maturing crops, okra seedling, corn seedling in high quantities, pest resistant crops, and provision of improved cassava stems. Some women in the groups also ventured into livestock production. The direct involvement of the populations-at-risk through the CPICs facilitated the selection, implementation, and monitoring of community-based initiatives. The project deliberately and thoughtfully included the women farmers, benefiting both the project and the farmers and strengthening the farmer networks. The team understood that farmers do not manage risk based solely on climate, but also consider market conditions, flow, costs, revenue, etc., and adjusted their strategies accordingly. This understanding ensured successful communication between the women farmers and the LGA representatives.

Women's committees' networks created and recognized by local government units. Empowered by their participation in the study and trainings, women farmers from the 10 communities organized themselves into committees (supported by the Intervention Council for Women in Africa [ICWA]) to exchange information and explore options for managing climate risk and diversifying economic activities. These networks have also become the voice for women in these communities, doing the slow work of advocating for women's inclusion in decision making and their right to own farmland. They also used life-skill building and income diversification models to support and encourage women to make change in their communities. As a result of this work, more women are promoting on-farm diversification, and men have become more receptive to women owning land and participating in household decision making. In some cases, women have taken the lead in ensuring that crop diversification features prominently in their farming household climate-change adaptation strategies. Many women in the region use new kinds of seeds, plant a greater variety of crops, and have found planting early maturing crops that do not require a lot of water, particularly maize, productive. Through crop diversification, farming households are able to spread risk over a wider range of crops, reducing their vulnerability to extreme weather events. Some women also diversify their economic activities, raising both crops and livestock, opening hair salons or small stores, or doing food sales. If the women can sustain these activities, their household incomes and food security would increase, reducing their vulnerability to climate change and building their capacity for adaptation. The establishment of these networks have created a voice for women in their communities as they advocate for women's inclusion in matters beyond climate change. The local authorities now recognize the network as a channel to distribute subsidized farm inputs to farming households because staff of the local authorities are also members of the network. Again, subsidies have been an alternative form of agricultural risk sharing.

# Project Outcomes 

How does the project contribute to the field of study / research area? Why was this research important to fund? What were the key scientific findings? What occurred as a result of this project?

This research was important to fund because:
Despite the Niger Delta region's high vulnerability to climate change, extensive research on the causes of such vulnerability, particularly the vulnerability of women, does not exist. The research that does exist has inadequate scientific data and has focused on isolated issues of environmental degradation. Little attention has been paid to rural communities, especially during the extended period of conflict and violence. Rural inhabitants of the Niger Delta, especially women, are highly vulnerable to the degradation of socio-economic conditions, social conflict, and climate-related disaster events because of their reliance on natural resources for subsistence, limited access to education and climate information, and poverty and inequality. Women are less likely than men to be able to influence policy or to be formal decision-makers themselves, and are less likely to receive an education, own land, and have access to welfare, credit, or formal employment. Moreover, additional family responsibilities (i.e., caregiving and securing food, water, and energy for cooking, heating, etc.) limit women's mobility. In fact, women are central to adaptation processes because of the significant roles they play in informal and semiformal strategies related to water and land management (e.g., access, collection, and treatment), sanitation, agriculture, environmental protection, and caregiving. Empirical research and on-the-ground implementation and validation are needed to understand and support the specific role these practices play in improving disaster risk reduction strategies. Further studies are also required to examine the patriarchal constraints to roles of women and their empowerment options in household in the ecological zones of Niger Delta region.

A key component of supporting development in fragile contexts, especially in Africa, is promoting research that can provide the basis for development planning. IDRC's role is crucial here, as weak governments in these regions cannot fund research.

## Key select findings

Climate change and economic vulnerability of the household in Niger Delta region
The findings show that most of the respondents live in precarious conditions and are intrinsically vulnerable to any shocks that affect their agricultural systems. As with most of the rural Niger Delta region, the respondents live in rustic houses, lack electricity and running water, own few assets, and rely on natural ecosystems for drinking water, firewood, wild foods, and materials for household construction. Agriculture is their mainstay for livelihoods, serving both as the primary source of household food and principal means of income generation. Consequently, Niger Delta farmers and fishers are particularly vulnerable to any reductions in crop productivity for a variety of reasons. First, they cultivate very small parcels of land (less than two acres), dedicate most of their land to crop production for household consumption, and obtain low crop yields, insufficient to meet household needs let alone provide surplus for sale. These low and declining yields reflect the limited use of inputs (fertilizers, pesticides, improved seed varieties, etc.), the lack of animal traction, low technology practices, suboptimal land for production, the prevalence of slash and burn crop production, and land degradation-all of which have been identified as constraints to agricultural productivity not only in the Niger Delta region but in other parts of Africa.

--- Page 7 ---

The results show that a majority of the households in all three ecological zones are chronically food insecure, which makes them extremely vulnerable to any climate or non-climatic shocks that further reduce agricultural production and food availability. Even in normal years, as revealed during interviews and focus groups, three-quarters of the farming households across the Niger Delta region spend, on average, 3.8 months without sufficient food. Food pressure is most acute in the months immediately prior to the main harvest season. The lack of sufficient food has significant livelihood impacts, including increased rates of malnutrition and child mortality.

Another factor that increases the vulnerability of the households is the remoteness of the villages and lack of adequate road infrastructure. In rural communities across the three ecological zones, roads are in a poor state and unevenly distributed, with many villages lacking roads to connect them to other villages. Even the main roads are often accessible only during the dry season. The livelihood implications of this isolation are significant, as farmers and other primary producers have difficulties getting their products to markets. They also have difficulty obtaining agricultural inputs and have to pay higher prices for them, reducing their profit margins.

A final set of factors that exacerbate household vulnerability is that most households lack access to formal safety nets to which they could turn in times of need. Most of the smallholder farmers remain outside a formal credit or banking system, lack capital, and are unable to access credit or loans. With no formal financial institutions, farmers must rely on informal support systems, borrowing money or food from family or friends. In addition, although there are numerous local NGOs working in the three regions, there is no formal extension service and less than $10 \%$ of the farmers currently receive any technical support. Farmers also have limited access to agro-meteorological or market information that could help inform farm management decisions, such as the choice of crops, planting dates, and management strategies, and which could serve as early warning systems for floods, rainstorms, and windstorms.

Gender-based assessment of climate-change adaptation strategies in Delta State, Nigeria
The results showed that more men than women farmers were likely to adopt and effectively use most climate-change impact adaptation strategies. While more men than women fishers were likely to adopt adaptation strategies, significantly more women than men fishers effectively made use of some strategies. The results also revealed that women farmers/fishers were constrained by limited access to education and weather/climate information, lower incomes, a lack of control of the use of farmlands, and non-involvement in decision making. It was recommended that gendered initiatives such as access to farm inputs, climate information, and training, and the provision of alternative source of energy, climate smart agriculture tools, and credit facilities for women be embedded into government and private development agencies' adaptation planning processes.

The project revealed that a majority of the women arable crop farmers were within their active age range, married, literate, had a family size of seven, and farmed less than one hectare of land with very limited extension contacts. A majority of the farmers employed early planting, crop rotation, and minimum tillage as strategies to combat the challenges of climate change. Damage to crops due to serious rains and overflow of watercourse bank resulted in significant losses, with the highest climate-related loss reported at N926,000.00 annually. The results indicate that age, marital status, level of education, farming expertise, access to credit, and contact with extension agents were all statistically vital to women arable crop farmers' expenditure on custom-made measures against

An unintended outcome was the results generated by the project on the impacts of the COVID-19 pandemic on local food production and informal food markets in Nigeria, with Niger

Delta region as case study (under CRFS COVID and Food systems stream 1). The team carried out a survey of 2,000 farmers (comprising 600 food producers, 600 food marketers, and 800 households) and found that disruptions to supplier and produce logistics, volatile markets, and financial measures arising from government responses to the COVID-19 outbreak had a significant impact on producers, greatly reducing profits and increasing the likelihood of a net loss in the first half of 2021. Coping strategies included adjusting stock management practices, finding new markets, and borrowing money. The results have been well summarized in these three policy briefs:

The effects of covid-19 containment measures on local food farmers in Delta state, Nigeria
The effects of covid-19 containment measures on local food marketing in Delta state, Nigeria
The effects of covid-19 containment measures on the welfare of household members and their livelihoods in Delta state, Nigeria

# Strategic Considerations 

Were the risk mitigation strategies outlined in the Project Approval Document effective? Were there any unanticipated risks that affected the project?

Unanticipated risks that affected the project were:
The COVID-19 pandemic made implementing activities difficult in Year 3 of the project. This was especially due to the restriction of movements and ban on gathering in person and holding face-to face meetings with policymakers. Consequently, planned activities had to be reorganized and reconsidered. The project team took time to adjust to the new normal in engaging key stakeholders following the outbreak of the COVID-19 pandemic as most of the senior researchers struggled to learn the new technologies for engaging in virtual meetings, group calls, and attending dialogues via video conferencing. The already huge connectivity issue in the Delta did not help the situation. Finally, COVID-19 brought into focus CPED's limited ability to facilitate interactions virtually among team members and between them and other stakeholders. While CPED was able to respond to this technological challenge by improving its facilities for virtual meetings and workshops, more improvements will be needed as COVID-19 is likely to continue to influence the mode of interactions with researchers and other stakeholders for years to come.

One challenge the institution had started to address are the preparations needed to ensure a smooth transition when the current Executive Director and founder of the CPED retires. The ED originally planned to retire in 2021, but will now retire this year, 2022. A successful leadership transition is critical to the CPED if it is to achieve its long-term objective of becoming a sustainable, policy-relevant, and action-research-oriented institution that aims to increase Nigerian policymakers' evidence-based knowledge and contribute to sustainable development across the country.

Are there any lessons arising from the assessment and management of research ethics in the project? You may wish to comment on institutional ethics review process efficacy and capacities; institutional and research team ethics oversight throughout the project; ethics training in the project; role of the IDRC program team and ACRE as appropriate; unforeseen ethical issues and how they were managed.

The research proposal and survey instruments were presented to the University of Benin Ethical Committee, the nationally recognized body for ethical approval in Delta State that is primarily covered by field survey activities in the project. Following the approval of the research protocol by the University, the Ministry of Environment wrote letters of permission to conduct the study to the key stakeholders in the 10 target LGAs, thus mobilizing preparations for the detailed field surveys. The project team then relocated to the 10 target LGAs to carry out project activities in full cooperation with all stakeholders involved.

Cultural constraints included a risk that women might be reluctant to hold open meetings with men, especially in the context of the Niger Delta region where women have faced years of violence. This risk was handled very carefully by ensuring efforts were made in rural communities to organize separate meetings for men and women and to ensure women were free to express views on issues affecting them. Risk of gender-based violence against women participants was curtailed by ensuring the project was understood by traditional leaders, elders, and men in target communities. Finally, all the community facilitators were from the Niger Delta, and this was crucial for thrust and to engage members in meaningful dialogue about the context and the issue or problem around which they are facilitating.
