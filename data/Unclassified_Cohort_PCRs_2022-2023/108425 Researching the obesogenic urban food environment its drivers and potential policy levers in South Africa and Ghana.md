# Project Highlights 

What are the five most important results (outputs or outcomes) of this project and how have they been useful or innovative?

Generated new knowledge and enhanced visibility:
This project resulted in 6 peer reviewed publications, 8 digital stories and 1 short documentary video. Collectively, these outputs have contributed to a stronger understanding of the factors contributing to obesogenic food environments in South Africa and Ghana. The project team also participated in several national and regional workshops and conferences, which helped establish their position as thought leaders in the field of food environment and health. For instance, the Ghanaian co-PI Dr Reggie Annan was appointed as one of the lead facilitators for Ghana's Food Systems Dialogue which was convened in the run up to the 2021 United Nations Food Systems Summit. The South African PI, Prof Rina Swart, was invited to serve in various advisory roles including projects led or funded by the Africa Center for Cities, UNICEF, and Stellenbosch University among others.

Deepened South-South research partnership
The project facilitated collaboration between researchers at the University of Western Cape and their counterparts at Kwame Nkrumah University of Science and Technology in Kumasi, Ghana. This type of South-South collaboration is crucial in fostering a community of practice in a previously under-researched field in Sub-Saharan Africa. A scoping study by IDRC's Food Environment and Health program found that research capacity in the food environment and health field was robust in South Africa but limited in the rest of the region. As a result, the program aimed to encourage collaborations between South African researchers and their counterparts in other African countries. The leadership and support provided by the South African team in this project serves as an effective and exemplary model for future collaborations.

Strengthened local research capacity:
The project provided support for 1 post-doctoral researcher (a female), 3 PhD students (including two females), and 2 master's students (one female). The research team's innovative methodologies were shared beyond the project through various workshops, such as the one offered at the International Congress of Dietetics on September 4, 2021, which had 35 attendees. The workshop focused on the project's insights and specifically the usefulness of qualitative methods in nutrition research.

Informed policy dialogues:
The project received recognition from governance and policy sectors in both study countries through strategic engagement with relevant platforms. Notably in Ghana, the project team co-convened the National Nutrition Dialogue in partnership with the National Development Planning Commission and other key players. The event was attended by the Minister of Planning, the Dutch Ambassador to Ghana, the President of the Ghana Academy of Nutrition and Dietetics, and featured a keynote address by the CEO of the John Agyekum Kufour Foundation. A communique was signed and distributed through media outlets, such as Modern Ghana.

In South Africa, the project team partnered with the Southern Africa Food Lab to establish a consortium of research and civil society organizations. The consortium organized a National Dialogue on Food Policy on October 31, 2019, in Tshwane, South Africa, bringing together 56 stakeholders from

government, civil society, academia, and the progressive food industry. The policy dialogue resulted in a broad consensus on the need for a more transparent and inclusive national food system, and a mandate for the consortium to continue advocating for this agenda.

Gender implications:
A gendered analysis showed that the gender of the person responsible for household food purchasing and preparation was linked to the types of food most frequently consumed. Households where men were responsible for purchasing food were found to consume more meat, fast food, salty snacks, and ready-to-eat meals, which have been linked to higher risks for non-communicable diseases. Meanwhile, households where women were responsible for food purchasing and preparation were found to consume more sugar and home-baked bread. These gender differences imply that health promotion and other behaviour change interventions must be tailored to each gender.

Project Outcomes
How does the project contribute to the field of study / research area?
Why was this research important to fund? What were the key scientific findings? What occurred as a result of this project?

This project has made a significant impact on the discourse around food systems and the vulnerability of the poor to unhealthy food consumption patterns both in Ghana and South Africa. The research findings confirmed the impact of modernization and urbanization on the shift towards processed foods and away from fresh produce and gained a better understanding of the underlying factors such as availability, cost, and infrastructure. Key findings from the analysis of food value chains included: limited access to healthy foods for the poor, a focus on profit over health in the value chain, challenges in the distribution of fresh produce, insufficient incentives for healthy food options from local government regulations, insufficient attention to real consumer needs in health promotion efforts, and an unequal playing field due to aggressive marketing of unhealthy foods.

This project engaged with and impacted over 200 stakeholders and individuals through learning journeys and multi-stakeholder meetings to address the challenges facing a sustainable food environment. Thus, the project has contributed to raising the profile and credibility of the research teams in the food policy space. For instance, the project team played a central role in the establishment of Ghana's first National Nutrition Forum, with a commitment by policymakers, civil society actors and other stakeholders to hold this forum annually. Additionally, members of the project team in South Africa were invited to contribute to revising the National Department of Health's Strategy on the Prevention of Obesity.
