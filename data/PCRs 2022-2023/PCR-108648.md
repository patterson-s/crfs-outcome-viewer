# Project Highlights

What are the five most important results (outputs or outcomes) of this project and how have they been useful or innovative?

Generated new knowledge:

This project resulted in 9 peer reviewed articles published as a special issue in the open access journal Global Health Action. With the support of the Conversation Africa, the project team also published 6 Op-Eds that have so far garnered over 5500 reads. Collectively, these publications provide evidence in support of fiscal policies for NCD prevention in Eastern and Southern Africa.

Strengthened local research capacity:

One of the specific objectives of this project was to strengthen local research capacity. Seven emerging researchers (1 per country) received technical support from the project team throughout the project duration. Further, three PhD candidates participated in the project and a fellowship was awarded to an MPH student to work on issues relating to SSB taxation.

Deepened South-South research partnership:

The project enabled the research team at PRICELESS SA to lead peer-learning among researchers across the seven study countries. These kinds of South-South collaborations are essential for catalysing a community of practice in what has hitherto been an under-researched field in Sub-Saharan Africa. The team at PRICELESS SA led by Prof Karen Hofman have established themselves as thought-leaders on the issue of fiscal policies for NCD prevention. Their previous work (also funded by IDRC: project 108424) informed South Africa's tax on sugar sweetened beverages (known officially as the Health Promotion Levy). This project was therefore a logical next step in terms of enabling the PRICELESS SA team to share their experiences and expertise with colleagues outside South Africa.

Gender implications:

The project team paid specific attention to gender considerations in their analysis of NCD policy content. Specifically, the team found that despite high-level policy commitments to gender mainstreaming across the seven study countries, gender was often neglected in specific policy documents such as national NCD prevention and control strategies. At the same time, the team found that there was a dearth of evidence on the gender burden of NCDs. On a positive note, interviews with key actors revealed a general sense that NCD policies should be gender-inclusive even though this remains to be seen in practice.

It is noteworthy that the project team had a very strong representation of women researchers. The PI Prof Karen Hofman and her core research team were all women including Safura Abdool Karim, Agnes Erzse, Petronell Kruger, Natasha Mazonde and Prof Anne-Marie Thow. Four of the seven emerging researchers were also women: Milkah Wajnohi (Kenya), Mulenga M. Mukanu (Zambia), Gemma Ahaibwe (Uganda), and Lebogang Gaogane (Botswana).

## Project Outcomes

How does the project contribute to the field of study / research area?

Why was this research important to fund? What were the key scientific findings? What occurred as a result of this project?

This project sought to generate evidence to inform the adoption of fiscal and policy interventions for addressing NCDs and their associated risk factors in seven African countries namely Botswana, Kenya, Namibia, Rwanda, Tanzania, Uganda, and Zambia. Specifically, the project aimed to assess data availability and quality on NCDs and associated modifiable risk factors; analyse the current policy landscape that would enable or challenge the prevention and control of NCDs; and describe the state of food industries that impact NCD burden (e.g. the sugar sweetened beverage industry). Collectively, this evidence was then to be used to develop recommendations on the most appropriate fiscal and policy interventions for NCD prevention in each study country. The findings of the project are as follows:

## NCD data is weak

Across the seven study countries, monitoring of NCD risk factors and their determinants was weak and often limited to large-scale household surveys such as Demographic and Health Surveys. Even so, these surveys did not adequately capture NCD risk factor indicators such as individual-level intake of unhealthy foods and beverages. Further, even where these indicators were available (e.g. through National STEPwise surveys), they tended to be outdated.

## NCDs are ignored outside of the health sector and in the media

NCDs were generally recognized in high-level policies such as national development plans, vision documents and nutrition/NCD strategies. This recognition was often accompanied by a call/mandate for multi-sectoral action on NCDs. However, this mandate was often not translated into sector-specific policies of non-health sectors such as agriculture and trade. Additional analysis of media coverage of NCDs showed that over a five-year period, there were only 35 media articles on SSB tax across the seven countries and a large portion of these articles related to South Africa's health promotion levy.

## Economic interests are at odds with NCD agendas

Conversations with policymakers and other key stakeholders revealed that industry actors (including the food and beverage industry) were framed as important to achieve economic growth and maintain employment opportunities, and there was generally high-level support for industry-led growth in

--- Page 3 ---

national development plans and other economic policy documents in all seven countries. Further analysis showed that industry actors publicly lobbied against SSB taxation (e.g. in Uganda reducing the tax and in Tanzania opposing increases) on the basis of the need to promote industry competitiveness.

Fiscal policies for NCD prevention are feasible

Across the seven study countries, the project found that there were no legal barriers to the adoption of fiscal policies such as SSB taxes. In fact, five countries already levied an excise tax on SSBs, either as a form of revenue generation or, in the case of Zambia, as a proposed public health measure. These existing taxes present an opportunity to advocate for the adoption of fiscal policies that both meet public health objectives and provide additional revenue to resource-constrained governments. However, to counter industry influence, the project recommends that NCD stakeholders need to: 1) shift the economic discourse e.g. by showing the economic impact of diagnosing and caring for people with NCDs; 2) forge links with other key non-health sectors such as agriculture sector in order to identify common policy objectives and benefits.

How would you compare the intended and actual results of the project? Why did it happen that way?

Were there any unintended outcomes of the project? Did the project evolve differently as detailed in the project approval document? Why did the unintended outcomes occur? What was the impact?

There were no unintended outcomes of this project. However, initially the project had intended to collect beverage industry data on market share and consumption trends. However, the project team discovered that publicly available industry data was very limited in their respective countries.

Consequently, the project team got approval from the responsible officer (Sam Oti) to reallocate funds to get industry data from paid sources such as Euromonitor. They were able to access purchased data on beverage companies for all countries except Rwanda and this information was included in the policy analysis work package of the project.

## Director comments

The relevant Program Division or Regional Director should provide specific comments on the project, and to formally approve the report and its contents.

The project analyzed the non-communicable disease policy ecosystem in seven countries of eastern and southern Africa. I note exemplary south-south collaboration including with field thought leaders and local research capacity strengthening of the next generation of NCD scientists. The work identified challenges of inadequate data on NCDs, siloed approach to address NCDs, which is limited to the health sector, and prioritization of economic concerns over NCDs prevention. The work makes recommendations related to these challenges including the costing of management of NCDS. The project also highlights the lack of gender concerns in NCD policies including an important knowledge gap on the gender burden of NCDs. These gaps continue to inform ongoing work on healthy food systems. PCR approved by Kathryn Toure, IDRC Regional Director, Nairobi, 25 October 2022
